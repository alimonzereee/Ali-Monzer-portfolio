## Overview

This project covers the design and simulation of a DC-to-AC power inverter based on a center-tapped transformer topology. This inverter architecture is commonly used in uninterruptible power supplies (UPS), DC-coupled renewable energy systems, and offline power conversion where galvanic isolation between the DC source and AC load is required.

## Topology Description

The center-tapped transformer inverter operates by alternately switching two transistors connected to either half of the transformer primary winding. This push-pull arrangement drives current through each half of the primary in alternating directions, inducing a sinusoidal (or quasi-sinusoidal) AC voltage on the secondary.

**Key characteristics:**
- Provides galvanic isolation between DC source and AC output
- Simpler gate drive circuitry compared to full-bridge topologies (no floating gate drives required)
- Transformer turns ratio sets the voltage step-up or step-down ratio

## Design Steps

1. **Transformer design** — Selection of turns ratio, core material, and winding configuration for the center-tapped primary and secondary
2. **Switch selection** — Power MOSFETs rated for peak voltage stress (twice the input voltage across the off-state switch)
3. **Gate drive design** — Complementary switching signals with dead-time to prevent simultaneous conduction
4. **Output filter** — LC filter on the secondary side to attenuate harmonics and deliver clean AC output

## Simulation & Analysis

The converter was simulated in **LTspice** and **Multisim** covering:
- Primary and secondary voltage and current waveforms
- Voltage stress on switching devices (2× Vdc)
- Output AC waveform quality and harmonic content
- Efficiency under various load conditions

## Technologies Used

- **LTspice** — Primary simulation environment
- **Multisim** — Cross-verification of switching waveforms
- **MATLAB** — Harmonic analysis of output waveform

## Key Takeaways

The center-tapped topology provides a practical balance between circuit simplicity and performance for medium-power isolated inverter applications. The voltage stress of twice the input voltage on each switch is the primary design constraint that dictates MOSFET selection and snubber circuit requirements.
